# Overview
Referencing Chapter 2 of [Practical Methods for Building System Architecture (IT Architects’ Archive Software Development Practices)](https://amzn.to/44JZInr), we will organize the definitions of architecture, architect, and architecting, along with their elements.

# Architecture is "Structure"
In IEEE 1471-2000, architecture is defined as follows:

> The fundamental structure of a system, realized through components, their relationships with each other or with the environment, and the fundamental principles guiding its design and evolution.

This definition indicates a perspective that views architecture as the **essential structure** of a system. What architecture targets is not superficial configuration diagrams, but a multifaceted structure involving relationships among components, behaviors, constraints, points of contact with the environment, and design criteria.

The characteristics of architecture are as follows:

- **Defines structure**: Clarifies what elements exist and how they relate.
- **Defines behavior**: Establishes how each element collaborates and functions as a system.
- **Focuses on critical elements**: Consciously concentrates on components and design decisions that will have long-term impacts.
- **Integrates and adjusts stakeholder needs**: Balances different interests to build an agreeable structure.
- **Is a synthesis of judgments based on logical reasoning**.
- **May follow existing architectural styles**: Such as layered, microservices, client-server, etc.
- **Is strongly influenced by the environment**: Constraints such as technology used, organizational structure, and operational requirements.
- **Affects the structure of the development organization**: Corresponding to Conway's Law.
- **All systems have architecture**: Even small ones have design intentions and structures.
- **Scope is always limited**: It does not necessarily encompass the whole, but has focus and purpose.

# What Does an Architect Do?
An architect is an individual, team, or organization responsible for the architecture (IEEE 1471-2000). Their responsibilities go beyond merely "deciding on configurations." Architects are required to possess a multifaceted set of abilities and qualities, such as:

- **Technical leader**: Leads the selection of structures and determination of technical direction.
- **Understands the software development process**: Familiar with the entire development lifecycle.
- **Has knowledge of the business domain**: Can bridge technology and business goals.
- **Is well-versed in technology**: Capable of conducting technology selection and evaluation.
- **Possesses design and programming skills**: Can assess the feasibility of structures.
- **Has excellent communication skills**: Can convey intentions and form agreements.
- **Has judgment**: Can choose the appropriate option from multiple choices.
- **Can navigate political considerations**: Manages interests both inside and outside the organization.
- **Has negotiation skills**: Derives feasible designs amidst constraints and demands.

An architect is more of a translator of intentions and structures than a code-writing expert, serving as a compass for development.

# What is Architecting?
Architecting refers to the series of actions related to defining, documenting, maintaining, improving, and assuring architecture. It is not merely a design task but has the following characteristics:

- **Requires a balance of science and art**: Necessitates both logical structural design and judgments based on experience and intuition.
- **Requires knowledge across multiple fields**: Familiarity with technology, processes, business, and management is desirable.
- **Is a continuous activity**: Design does not end with a single instance; it evolves in response to changes.
- **Is motivated by many stakeholders**: Needs to reflect diverse perspectives from customers, management, developers, etc.
- **Often involves trade-offs**: Adjustments between conflicting requirements such as performance and cost, quality and delivery time are essential.
- **Recognizes the importance of experience**: Many situations require pattern recognition and intuitive judgment.
- **Requires both top-down and bottom-up perspectives**: Needs to balance an overview of the structure with an understanding of on-the-ground realities.

# Benefits of Architecting
Architecting offers several practical benefits, including:

- **Improved system quality**
- **Facilitation of consensus among stakeholders**
- **Support for mid- to long-term planning**
- **Maintenance of structural consistency**
- **Management of system complexity**
- **Increased reusability**
- **Reduction of maintenance costs**
- **Improved accuracy of impact analysis during changes**

Architecting can be seen as a "structural investment to prevent future chaos."

# Conclusion
Architecture is not just a blueprint, but a **product that reflects the intentions of stakeholders and elevates them into a coherent structure**. The architect is both the designer of that structure and a facilitator who translates intentions and judgments, guiding the whole.

Through this chapter, we have been able to enhance the resolution of the terms "architecture," "architect," and "architecting."
