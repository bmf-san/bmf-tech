# Overview
There are times when I submit a CFP for speaking events like conferences, so I thought I would write a poem about how I write and submit my CFPs.

# Past Achievements
I have submitted a total of 7 CFPs in the past, with a 100% acceptance rate (one was declined due to various circumstances).

The events I spoke at were PHP conferences and Go conferences, with more appearances at PHP conferences.

cf. https://speakerdeck.com/bmf_san

To be honest, there were many shortcomings in the content of my early presentations and CFPs when I first started speaking, but I have gained quite a bit of confidence recently, so I thought I would leave some thoughts on the matter.

# My Style of Writing a CFP
## Before Writing a CFP...
I want to organize what I always do before writing a CFP.

### Consider the Purpose of Speaking
What do I want to gain from speaking? Knowing this motivates me to present.

I am conscious of whether it is something that can be valuable for both myself and the audience. I don't think it's bad to only consider the benefits for myself and think that the audience finds value in my acceptance, but I believe that considering various perspectives broadens the range of presentation themes and increases what I can gain.

### Decide on the Event to Speak At
If it's an event I've attended in the past, I think it's easier to have an image of the atmosphere and themes, but for events I haven't attended, I think it's better to research past events or actually participate in them.

Knowing what themes and levels of presentations exist, as well as the characteristics of the audience and sponsoring companies, can help build confidence when I actually present, so I consider this a crucial preparation step.

I personally don't feel nervous at all about speaking in front of people due to my past experiences; rather, I enjoy it. However, I believe that completing this preliminary research contributes to maintaining my mental state.

### Carefully Check the Event Schedule
This is also a common point, but it would be sad if the schedule doesn't match even after the CFP is accepted, so it's good to finalize schedule adjustments in advance.

## How to Write a CFP
By the time I submit the CFP, I generally have a clear image of the presentation, which might be my approach.

When I start writing the CFP, I have the outline of the presentation theme somewhat organized and am almost ready to start writing the slides.

It may vary from person to person, but in my case, I usually have the "topic" prepared in advance and consider speaking only after I see that "this topic seems presentable". (There are cases where I create topics from keywords and prepare for presentations, which can be driven by the presentation itself or something close to it.)

I will explain step by step how I submit a CFP.

### Prepare the Topic
Nothing starts without a topic.

Sometimes I take what I have been doing privately based on my interests and concerns directly as a topic, or I may start thinking about a topic related to a specific technology, but most of the time it's the former.

### Write a Blog
It may seem strange to write a blog to submit a CFP, but this process is very important for me.

Writing a blog ≒ I can write the CFP and create slides.

By completing the blog, I can expand on the topic, making the writing of the CFP and the creation of slides go smoothly.

It's important to write the blog with a proper structure, not just as a memo.

### Write the CFP
My method is to write the CFP after writing the blog.

Instead of jumping straight into writing the CFP, I organize various things to prepare for it.

Recently, I have been using Miro for organization.

I organize it like the image below.

![miro-cfp](https://user-images.githubusercontent.com/13291041/228522378-8666892b-aaa3-4d30-8bfa-063af494225d.png)

The format I prepare to write the CFP includes:

- Session Title
- Event Information
- CFP
- Acceptance Criteria
- What to Talk About / What Not to Talk About
- Key Points
- Keywords
- Outline
- Rough Slides
- Research

By completing this format in no particular order, the CFP is finished.

The original idea for this format is based on what I learned from a senior engineer at a company I used to work for, but I have added my own touches.

Using the preparation content I submitted for Go Conference 2021 as an example, I will explain each part of the format.

#### Session Title
Write the session title.

When I have the topic prepared and the blog written, I can generally determine the session title, but if those are not yet prepared, I may decide on a provisional title first.

In terms of thinking about the title, I only consider whether it can convey the image of the presentation content in one breath. This is similar to the thought process of naming functions or variables when coding.

![Session Title](https://user-images.githubusercontent.com/13291041/228795335-2a813b7e-8412-43cf-bf38-64f8a8b7d255.png)

#### Event Information
Check and write down information about the event.

Include the event title, event date and time, and details about the speaking slot for which you are submitting the CFP.

![Event Information](https://user-images.githubusercontent.com/13291041/228795322-902f4835-ced1-4f97-84ff-e79483aee9cb.png)

#### CFP
Write the text for the CFP you are submitting.

Sometimes I write it after organizing the other parts, and sometimes I write a rough draft first.

I feel that the CFP has a certain word limit, but even if it doesn't, I try to write as concisely as possible.

I believe that if I include the necessary information for the reviewers, I should be able to write it briefly.

I write the CFP with the following outline:

- What I will talk about
- Who I want to talk to or who would benefit from listening
- What information can be gained from the presentation
- Outline of the presentation content
- Link to supplementary materials

![CFP](https://user-images.githubusercontent.com/13291041/228795290-a0f50267-6edc-4d02-b04d-e4bbe0abf6b5.png)

#### Acceptance Criteria
Check and write down the acceptance criteria for the event.

Most conferences have established acceptance criteria.

This is a regulation, so it should be read and confirmed carefully.

In addition to the acceptance criteria, the code of conduct should also be read thoroughly.

![Acceptance Criteria](https://user-images.githubusercontent.com/13291041/228795338-5a2deeb6-d343-4571-9cc4-5053484d699c.png)

#### What to Talk About / What Not to Talk About
Write down what you will talk about and what you will not talk about.

It is particularly important to clearly state what you will not talk about. If you prioritize the main theme you want to convey and end up talking too much about unrelated topics, it can make the content diffuse and hard to convey, so it's important to clarify what doesn't need to be discussed.

![What to Talk About / What Not to Talk About](https://user-images.githubusercontent.com/13291041/228795346-9ea4cec0-5ae6-4239-a043-4efb8fad68de.png)

#### Key Points
Write down what you want to convey.

I think it's fine to write as many as you want, but it's better to focus on one point per theme to keep the content coherent.

![Key Points](https://user-images.githubusercontent.com/13291041/228795341-06470fad-c4a6-4a48-8e86-6c4a5cec4af3.png)

#### Keywords
Pick and write down keywords related to the presentation content.

This is more about organizing and finding key points rather than expanding ideas.

By writing them out, it becomes clear which keywords are important, making it easier to summarize what I want to convey and my arguments.

Conversely, it also reveals keywords that may not need to be touched upon, so I try to write them out as an exercise.

![Keywords](https://user-images.githubusercontent.com/13291041/228795328-93c629d2-13c9-4115-ab97-c7acd22d6355.png)

#### Outline
Write the outline of the presentation content.

I sometimes write this and sometimes don't. If the blog is finished, I may not write it, and if it isn't, I may write it to prepare for writing the blog.

If the blog is finished, I organize the outline based on the blog content.

![Outline](https://user-images.githubusercontent.com/13291041/228795315-f9749517-c0d2-4400-9482-681b0ea350ba.png)

#### Rough Slides
Write a rough image of the slides based on sticky notes.

This is to visualize how to translate the outline into slides, but recently I have been starting the slide creation software and creating the outline as sticky notes in the slides, so it may not be necessary. However, doing this allows me to be aware of the flow of the presentation, helping me notice any awkwardness or areas that need to be explored further, so I think it's worth doing.

#### Research
Write down anything you want to investigate thoroughly or delve into later.

Summarize research items such as preparing sample implementations or conducting technical verifications.

I also write down confirmation items such as the logic or fact-checking of what I want to assert.

# Summary
The key point or tip for me when writing a CFP is to "have the blog written up".

If I can output in writing, I feel like I have completed about 60-70% of the presentation preparation.

This is how I usually write a CFP, and while my methods may become more relaxed over time, I make sure not to skip the task of writing a blog.

Once the CFP is accepted, I can smoothly proceed with creating and refining the slides.

Although there are some formal aspects, I believe that not neglecting preparation leads to smoother work and a higher acceptance rate (in my personal opinion).

By the way, there was one event where I served on the acceptance side as an organizer, and I think it's also important to understand the feelings of the reviewers.

## Things I Keep in Mind When Creating Slides
Here are some points I keep in mind when creating slides for speaking events:

- First, finish a rough draft; just get your hands moving first, and refine the details gradually. Agile-like.
  - If you try to complete one slide at a time, you might fall into the waterfall trap.
    - Generally, this leads to rework or losing sight of the overall flow of the slides.
- Don't put in too much text.
  - I believe presentation materials should be like that.
  - Even if you write a lot of text, the audience won't be able to read it all, and they come to listen to the talk.
- Practice.
  - Measure time, practice in the same environment as the actual presentation (devices, presentation methods, etc.).
    - Make sure to operate the slide transitions properly and check if the speaker notes are visible if you are doing screen splitting.
    - If you struggle during the actual presentation, you waste time...
    - It’s also good to roughly check how many seconds you can spend per slide.
- Keep the volume moderate.
  - For a 15-minute presentation, it’s good to limit the number of slides and content to what can be covered in about 12-13 minutes.
  - Even if you’re not nervous, if you have a lot to say, the talk tends to extend (which can also be due to lack of practice).
- Don’t write too much in speaker notes.
  - Don’t make notes that can be read verbatim during the presentation.
    - Unless you’re an announcer, it tends to sound monotonous.
- Think about the words to use at the transitions of the talk.
  - There are moments in the flow of the talk where the topic switches, and I often struggle with how to smoothly transition to the next topic, so I prepare my word choices.
    - If I can’t find good words, it might indicate that there’s room for improvement in the flow of the talk, so I refactor it.
- Keep the self-introduction slide simple.
  - Unless it’s necessary to write in detail based on the purpose of the presentation or the event, keep it brief.
  - There’s a strategic approach to writing it as a way to communicate with someone during after-parties, but that can be considered after all the slides are completed.
- Avoid easy jokes or gags.
  - In official talks, don’t casually include “gags.” There’s no need to take on unnecessary risks that don’t match the cost.
    - Avoid quirky metaphors as well...
- Get reviews.
  - I often don’t do this as an individual activity, but in the context of work, it’s good to get feedback through reviews from others.
